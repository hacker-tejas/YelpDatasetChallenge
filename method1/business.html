        First of all, I love that the old Cass Corridor, now called Midtown, has a new place that is aptly named "Standard"; as the Selden Standard will certainly be the standard for all other places that open after it.

        This place was amazing!  I think the space was an old defunct laundromat (am I remembering that right?) but there is certainly nothing remaining of that eyesore.  Rather, this is a contemporary oasis with an open kitchen, wood burning oven, subway tile all around, a large bar and chefs table as well as a nice seating area.

        While they take reservations, they only do so for about 30% of the seats for their wish is to be the "neighborhood place" allowing for the majority of seats to be walk ins, which is a concept that I loved.

        And this place is busy!  I went on a Wednesday night at about 5:30 thinking we would beat the rush and get a table.  Boy was I wrong!  The place was already packed (all the cars parked up and down 2nd ave should have been my clue) and the hostess told me that it would be a 45 min to an hour wait for a table.  Luckily for me and my mom who was with me- there were a couple of seats at the bar which we quickly grabbed.  And the best news was that shortly after sitting at the bar, the hostess came and moved us to a table (you have no idea how happy that made my mom who had never eaten at a bar in her life!!)

        Our server, Anna,  was over in a flash and she was great.  She was helpful in explaining the menu and making sure everything was just the way we wanted it.  We started with the oysters and the grilled octopus both of which were good and I loved the mignonette sauce with it's peppery taste that was served with the oysters.

        For our entrees, I went with the seafood stew and my mom had the pork confit.  The seafood stew is new on the menu and is a wonderful mix of mussels, squid and 4 large grilled shrimp in a tomato vegetable base served with bread and aioli.  What a wonderful dish!  And the pork was tender and served with spatzele.

        If you go- save room for dessert.  The banoffee is a must have and calorie worthy all the way around.

        If you go- there is plenty of free street parking on 2nd street.  Handicapped parking is along the alley side of the building.  And the prices are very reasonable with entrees in the $20 range and appetizers in the mid teens.  Dinner for two with no drinks was just under $100.

        I love that this area is coming back from some tough times.  You need only look at the lovely Victorian buildings surrounding the area to see what this area once was like and will be again.  If Selden Standard is the standard now for the area, the area is in a very, very, good place.

        Yelp has plenty of excellent reviews describing - accurately - how delicious the food is.  It is no mistake that Selden Standard has been named the Detroit Free Press 2015 restaurant of the year.  Long story short - the food is great. So, I want to concentrate my attention on the immaculate, outstanding, make-your-day service that I experienced.  

        I was in Midtown for a meeting that ended early and I decided on the spur of the moment to visit Selden Standard, which I'd been meaning to try for months.  I arrived just as the bar opened at 4:30pm.  Feeling a wee bit sheepish because I was alone, I asked if they could accommodate a single diner at 5pm.  Not only could they accommodate me, the host assured, but since I was there alone, I should sit at the chef's counter so I could enjoy watching the meals being prepared.  She also explained that patrons without reservations would be seated in the order they arrived, so by looking around me, I could deduce I would be seated in the 2nd handful of people.  Nice to know.

        I grabbed a spot at the bar to while away the half hour, and enjoyed a delicious craft cocktail called "The Burden of Speech"; a mixture of apple brandy, Michigan plum shrub (plum flavored drinking vinegar with simple syrup), amaretto, and lemon.  YUM!  I sat in the corner, happily engaged with my drink and my device, and neither the bartender nor the bar back nor anyone at all looked askance at my device-y-ness.

        About five minutes before 5pm, the bartender asked if I would be remaining at the bar. Upon learning that I would be moving to the chef's counter, he cashed me out just in time for the host to seat me.  She seated me one chair away from a much younger guy on my left, who also looked to be on his own, and on my right, she seated a man about my age, and also flying solo, right next to me (as I was soon to realize, this seating arrangement was no coincidence).

        A couple minutes after seating me, the host came back up and, addressing me by my name, asked me if it was OK for her to put my forgotten-at-the-bar jacket on the back of the chair. Nice!

        I was now in the capable hands of my server, Mikhail, who soon made it clear why I had been seated right next to the gentleman to my right.  Instead of addressing us separately, he engaged in heavy-handed diner matchmaking, letting each of us hear what the other ordered, and then suggesting that we share bites with each other.  Mikhail hit just the right tone, and soon my impromptu dinner-mate (Bob), and I, both foodies as it turned out, were talking up a blue streak about restaurants all around the metro-Detroit area, and sharing our plates. Wow!  Socks officially blown off.

        Just for the record, here's what we ate:  Roasted beets with ricotta, pomegranate and walnuts; sweet potato ravioli in brown butter with sage and pecans; seared scallops in brown butter raisin sauce with cauliflower and and almonds; and polenta al forno with tomato mushroom sauce, shaved fontina and arugula.  Each dish was a revelation, with flavors perfectly paired and seasoning just so.  

        After making the match, Mikhail let the meal take its course and, when we were finished, discreetly served each of us our own bill.  

        I went to Selden Standard expecting only to experience wonderful food.  But thanks to the wonderful, thoughtful service, my entire experience was fabulous.

        So I dropped by again tonight, and I can surely say that this will be one of the few spots I'll come to more regularly.

        The restaurant opened at 4:30 pm today and they start seating patrons at 5 pm. There was a two-hour wait when I walked in the door, but good thing I called ahead and was able to secure a seat at the chef's bar.

        I decided to branch out this time and instead of eating the heavier plates, I opted for the lighter ones -- oysters, beef tartare, and mussels.

        The oysters were delicious with the mignonette (would get again). Their beef tartare isn't your usual french recipe with potatoes or pommes frites (mustard, capers, shallots, etc). They get their beef from the farm up at Michigan State University (go green, go white!) This dish has a kick to it with some type of chili, and a little heavier on the onion and garlic. It kind of overpowered the beef, but it was still delicious and it didn't stop me from eating it. The herb salad that came with the beef tartare was really good too, loved the acidity, but a little heavy on the salt. Nothing that can't be tweaked. Good dish. Now, the mussels. It was good, but different. A little heavier than your classic mussels in white wine sauce. This dish had chorizo, beans, and some lime. This added more depth to the sauce and made it more savory yet it was still light with the squeeze of lime.

        For dessert, I was going to order the tiramisu but my waitress told me the banoffee tart is their most popular dessert. Personally, I hate bananas, but I thought "if everyone loves it, why not?" This piece of heaven has a thick but not too heavy of a caramel sauce with a whipped vanilla cream and caramelized bananas (think of creme brûlée). Let me reiterate: I haaaate bananas in my dessert. I took three bites and it was so good I had to share the love, so I shared it with a couple that was sitting next to me, who happened to be foodies. One of them doesn't like bananas and they tried it and seemed to like it. Great flavors and textures going on throughout this dessert.

        I waved Mike (the general manager) over to me and I literally couldn't say anything because the banoffee tart was taking over me.  We made eye contact and he already knew I was in that banoffee trance. Anyway, he came over and we got to talking about my meal, and Detroit.  Real nice guy, awesome staff, I have nothing but great things to say about this restaurant, and I can't wait to come back and try more dishes.

The dishes came out randomly as they were ready, and while they varied in size, there was always at least a couple of bites per person.

        First of all, there was very little thought given to plating, but whatever... it's the taste that really counts, right? For example: grilled octopus is a staple in South America -- we call it "pulpo a la parrilla," and it's one of the dishes with which you can truly judge a restaurant's merit -- but SS's idea of "charred" was to scorch baby octopus with fried oil so that it tasted like a bougie fishstick. I kinda want people to, like, know that it doesn't have to be that way. Everything else was equally disappointing or unmemorable.

        For dessert, we ordered the banoffee tart and chocolate pot de creme. The tart was another one-note wonder and the chocolate was probably the single most uninspired dessert I have ever eaten. If jello pudding is Bill Cosby, it tasted like the current washed-up criminal version.

I don't think a startup in Detroit needs to be on par with a tapas bar in Spain, but you can get better... oh, let's say ragu... at a place like Mani Osteria in Ann Arbor.

        I won't go back, and I really wouldn't recommend it.
